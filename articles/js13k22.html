Before the jam

Since js13kgames 2021, I've made a few games:

- IT'S TOO RAW!, my entry for GamedevJS 2022 (PLAY / Github)
- MiniWordle, a Wordle clone in 214 bytes (PLAY / Github)

... and tools:

- MiniMidi, to easily extract notes from a MIDI file (DEMO / Github)
- Midi2Array, a more complete tool to extract notes from MIDI files (DEMO / Github)
- W, a WebGL2 micro-framework developed with @stas (DEMO / Github)
- Zipzop, a tool to compress a file with Zip and Zopfli, online (DEMO / Github)
- MiniCode, to easily find the event.code of Keyboard events (DEMO / Github)
- Unicode13, lists and tools for Unicode 13.0 glyphs and emoji (Github)
- Mini GLSL minifier, a micro WebGL 1.0/2.0 shader minifier made with 10 regexes (DEMO / Github)

10 Years already!

I also remarked that in 2022, I've been Code Golfing in JS for 10 years. Let's celebrate it!

I've made a list of all the micro games I had created or contributed to since 2012,
and took the challenge to put them all in a single js13kgames entry.

It took me two weeks to secretly select, fix, resize, clean up and regolf a total of 28 games and 2 emulators (CHIP-8 and NES), and present them in the form of a book with the source code on the left and the playable demo on the right.
The content is so dense that it required 4 "hard" passes of RoadRoller, Zip, and Zopfli to make the entry fit into 13300 bytes.

The full readme with sources and instructions for each game is available on Github.

The less easy part was to create dynamic iframes with sometimes astral Unicode characters in their source code, focus them, make them refreshable, etc. but I finally manage to do everything as elegantly as possible.

Days 1 & 2: the theme

On August 13th, the theme dropped: "DEATH". Hm, tough one.

The problem was that none of my ideas could really match this theme.
So I started scribbling some artwork, to seek inspiration...

But nothing clicked.

I knew I didn't want to remake an existing game like Karoshi (where you have to die to go to the next level) or Life Goes On (where you need to use your past corpses to reach the exit of each level).
And it's been a good decision: 2 other entries used these mechanics and they are very well done.

Finally, I realized that I could interpret DEATH as a game that takes place in some sort of afterlife place, like a purgatory, where time and space as we know them don't really exist.
And that could be a good reason to be able to invert time (like in TENET) but also gravity.
So I started storyboarding an intro scene to present this in a fun way:

I showed it to some friends, they said it was fun, so I decided to get started on this idea!

By the way: cheers to Salvatore Previtti (author of Dante) and Adrien Guéret (author of Terror Defense), who were there during all the development of my entry.
We brainstormed, shared tips, beta-tested, joked and heped each other stay kind of motivated during all this month, and God knows it hasn't been easy!

Speaking of which, there's a reference to Salvatore in my entry and a reference to me in his entry, as a souvenir of how we helped each other during this month...

Days 3 & 4: do the intro while brainstorming...

As it wasn't yet super clear for me what my game would look like and contain, I started animating my intro sequence, without music.

I know I wanted the graphics to be original compared to my last entries, so instead of pixel art or CSS3D, I drew everything in SVG (with my tool MiniSVGCreator) and CSS gradients, and animated it using CSS transitions and JS timeouts.

It's only towards the end of this art exploration that I decided that my game would be monochrome, like LIMBO and INSIDE.

Here's all the art I've drawn in SVG: characters, sweat drop, evil eyes, thunder, fire, speech bubbles. (2kb minified / 0.7kb zipped)... (I ended up only using the characters.)

Days 7 & 8: Platform engine

It's time to start working on some interactivity now!

As in all my 2D platform games, I started implementing the gravity, then the ground collisions, then the jumps, then the collisions on all four sides of the character.

There is one thing that made the development of this platform engine much harder though: the character is taller than one block of the map. So even when he walks, its head and its feet are aligned with different "slices" of the map. During jumps, it can even touch 3 different blocks.

This design choice made me implement a 8-point hit box around the character...
(and had me spend countless hours debugging my code)
